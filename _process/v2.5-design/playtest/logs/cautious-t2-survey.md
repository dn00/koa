## Q1: Strategy Discovery

I figured out a few things the rules did not explain:

- **Corroboration bonuses**: Playing two cards with the same tag in one turn gives a damage bonus. If those cards come from different devices (sources), the bonus is bigger (I saw 30% vs 20%). This was never stated but became clear from the math in run 2.
- **Repetition penalty**: Playing a card that proves the same proof type as something I already submitted adds extra scrutiny. I noticed this in run 1 when microwave (ALERTNESS) triggered a repetition warning because fitbit already proved ALERTNESS.
- **Counter-evidence and refutation**: The security camera was CONTESTED until I played the doorbell, which refuted the counter. Ordering matters -- refute first, then play the strong card.
- **Tag conflicts**: AWAKE and ASLEEP are opposing tags. I was told cards can conflict by tags, so I stayed away from AWAKE cards entirely. It felt safer to never touch them.

## Q2: Adaptation

Run 1 was pure caution -- all zero-risk cards, one card at a time when possible. I lost with 2 resistance remaining. The math just was not there.

For run 2, I realized I needed the security camera's power (5), but it was contested. So I played doorbell first to refute the counter, then paired fitbit + security_cam for the corroboration bonus. That was the key insight: a little risk (risk 1 on doorbell and security_cam) was necessary to generate enough damage.

For run 3, I tried to address more concerns by pairing doorbell + thermostat on turn 1 (both HOME, which gave a corroboration bonus I did not expect). Then the same fitbit + security_cam combo on turn 2 sealed it. I wanted to address the INTENT concern too, but the only card for that was the speaker (AWAKE tag), and I was not willing to risk a contradiction after committing ASLEEP with the fitbit.

## Q3: KOA

KOA's responses influenced me somewhat. The "you keep circling back to that same point" line in run 1 made me realize I was proving the same thing twice (ALERTNESS via both fitbit and microwave). That was a real "oh, I should diversify my proofs" moment.

The "Fine. That checks out. I'll drop that line of questioning" when I played doorbell was satisfying -- it confirmed the refutation worked and made me feel like the counter-evidence system was worth engaging with.

KOA felt more like a character than a raw game system, but only barely. The voice lines were fun, but I could see the system behind them. "Two different devices agreeing? That's annoyingly consistent" was my favorite -- it rewarded smart play with personality.

## Q4: Enjoyment

4 out of 5. I genuinely enjoyed the puzzle-solving. The first loss taught me something, and the subsequent wins felt earned. The only reason it is not a 5 is that 3 runs on the same puzzle started feeling solved by run 3.

## Q5: Replay

Yes, I would play a different puzzle. The core mechanics -- corroboration, contradictions, refutation ordering -- are interesting enough to carry new scenarios. I would especially like to see a puzzle where the "safe" path is not so clearly separated from the "risky" path. Here, the AWAKE cards were obviously traps for a cautious player. A scenario where you have to take a calculated risk would be more interesting.

## Q6: Confusion

- The **repetition risk** mechanic was not obvious. In run 1, I did not understand why playing microwave after fitbit added scrutiny until I reasoned about it afterward. The briefing said nothing about proof types repeating. KOA's line about "circling back" helped, but I had to infer the mechanic.
- The **corroboration bonus** was completely hidden. I only noticed it because the damage numbers did not add up to what I expected. A hint that "consistent evidence is stronger" would have been welcome without spoiling the mechanic.
- I was never sure if **concerns** mattered for winning. I addressed 2/3 in my wins and still got CLEAN badge. Are concerns just for a higher badge? That was unclear.
